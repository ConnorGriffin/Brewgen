# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Non-expert homebrewers describing a beer they want to taste — extract-kit
brewers going custom for the first time, hobbyists who live in tools like
Brewfather, and veteran all-grain brewers who want fast comparison, not
instruction. They arrive with flavor words ("malty, a little honey, no
coffee"), not sensory-model numbers.

## Product Purpose

Brewgen turns a beer-style and flavor brief into a set of distinct fermentable
grain bills (see CONTEXT.md for canonical language). Success: a non-expert
states a brief in one compact screen, compares the generated grain bills at a
glance, and walks away knowing exactly what grain to buy — without reading
prose or decoding charts.

## Brand Personality

Editorial, tactile, unhurried. A well-set brew-book page, not a dashboard:
serif display type, warm cream/amber/ink/rosewood, malt rendered as physical
layers. Words over widgets; the app reads your brief back as a sentence.

## Anti-references

- Dashboard furniture: spreadsheet grids, badge pills, radar/scatter charts,
  comparison matrices, analytical framing (all rejected in issue #26 rounds).
- Generic generated-UI aesthetics: evenly-weighted everything, tiny labels,
  forced charts.
- Prose walls. "AI loves writing it, users hate reading it."

## Design Principles

- Composition is the picture: the grain bill itself (real malt colors,
  proportional layers) is the primary visual, never an abstract chart.
- No ranking: letters identify grain bills; layout must not smuggle in a
  winner.
- Sparse differentiation: annotate only meaningful differences, in words.
- Numbers a brewer trusts: exact percentages, pounds, OG, SRM — visible, not
  buried; qualitative words for non-experts sit beside them, not instead.
- Advanced depth stays folded: equipment and model assumptions live under a
  quiet disclosure, defaulted intelligently.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA. Color never the only encoding (malt stacks always paired with the
textual percentage list); keyboard-reachable cards, spines, and word-step
controls; both light and proposed dark themes hold contrast.
